# Product-Sense Interview Answer

A scaffold that sounds strong out loud, prevents solution-jumping, forces real prioritization, and leaves the interviewer a clean story to follow. Not a memorize-and-recite script.

## What these interviews actually test

Whether you can: clarify an ambiguous prompt without freezing, tie user value to market or business logic, segment instead of saying "everyone", prioritize one pain point instead of listing ten, make MVP tradeoffs explicit, and communicate clearly under time pressure.

## The six-part spine

Order matters. Skip from prompt to features and the answer sounds clever but ungrounded. Establish user, goal, and pain first and the solution feels earned.

1. **Clarify.** Reduce ambiguity, define scope, state assumptions. Ask only 1 to 2 questions; more feels like stalling.
2. **Rationale.** Why does this problem matter now, for the market and (if relevant) the company?
3. **Product goal.** The user outcome you want to create, before any features.
4. **Segmentation.** Who you serve first, and why that target wins. Keep segments distinct.
5. **Pain points.** Map the journey, name the main frictions, pick the one worth solving first. Use reach, frequency, and severity to choose.
6. **Solution.** Generate distinct options (not three versions of one), compare them, commit to one MVP with explicit exclusions. Use impact, fit, and effort to justify the cut.

## Delivery rules

- State your structure up front so the interviewer knows where you are going.
- Keep lists MECE: distinct segments, non-overlapping pain points, genuinely different solutions.
- Speak in short sentences. It should sound conversational, not like a memo read aloud.
- If no company is named, assume a startup and skip fake mission talk.
- When the interviewer redirects or narrows scope, adapt the spine; do not plow through a script.

## Watch for

- Feature brainstorming with no chosen user or problem.
- A TAM presentation with invented numbers instead of product sense.
- A business-case-only answer with no empathy, behavior, or user context.

## Not for

Behavioral interviews (use STAR stories), execution and analytics cases (metrics diagnosis), or go-to-market and pricing interviews where distribution or monetization is the core problem.
